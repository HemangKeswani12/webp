01 / VISION
PHILOSOPHY

The Human Element in Engineering

I believe the energy crisis isn't just a resource problem—it's an engineering challenge waiting for a human-centric solution. My work focuses on the intersection of sustainable energy management, specifically harnessing the immense potential of nuclear power, and pioneering space-based energy production systems like Dyson swarms.


From the quiet corridors of research institutes to the high-pressure environment of international debates, I've learned that technology without empathy is hollow. Whether I'm redesigning laptop cooling systems to extend hardware lifespans or leading teams to design orbital settlements for 10,000 people, my goal remains the same: to build systems that integrate seamlessly into the fabric of our daily lives, making the complex accessible and the ambitious achievable.

02 / RESEARCH & INNOVATION
PHYSICS & ENERGY

CERN Research Proposal

Collaborating with the Physics Department at IISER Pune, I delved into the complexities of high-energy physics. Our proposal for CERN wasn't just about theoretical particles; it was an attempt to bridge the gap between fundamental physics and practical energy applications.


The process involved rigorous networking, interdisciplinary coordination, and a deep dive into the mechanics of the universe. It taught me that the answers to our sustainable energy future might be hidden in the smallest building blocks of matter.

AEROSPACE ENGINEERING

ARSSDC Space Settlement

At the Asian Regional Space Settlement Design Competition, we weren't just building a model; we were designing a society. As the runner-up team among 150 schools, I led the systems engineering effort for a viable orbital habitat.


Balancing the cold, hard constraints of aerospace engineering with the soft, chaotic needs of human psychology, sociology, and economics was a lesson in holistic design. We engineered life support systems, gravity simulation, and social structures, proving that living among the stars requires as much empathy as it does rocket science.

AUTOMOTIVE ENGINEERING

F1 in Schools (Doppler Racing)

Speed is a function of precision. Leading the engineering division for Doppler Racing, I secured the National Best Engineer Award. We designed a miniature F1 car that covered 20 meters in just 1.144 seconds.


This achievement was the result of months of iterative design, Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) analysis, and wind tunnel testing. It wasn't just about making it fast; it was about understanding airflow, reducing drag, and manufacturing with sub-millimeter tolerance.
